Core Security Principles

Physical security follows the same defence-in-depth logic as cyber security. No single control is sufficient — layered, overlapping controls ensure that defeating one layer does not compromise the whole.

Section 03

Physical Control Categories

Every physical security control falls into one of four functional categories. Effective architecture requires all four — deterrents reduce likelihood, preventive controls stop entry, detective controls identify breaches, and corrective controls restore security.

🛡️
Preventive controls

Stop an attack or intrusion from occurring. Physical barriers that an attacker must defeat before reaching an asset.

  • Perimeter fencing and walls
  • Mantraps and airlocks
  • Anti-ram bollards and barriers
  • Reinforced doors and locks
  • Access control systems (PACS)
  • Safe and vault storage
👁️
Detective controls

Identify and alert on security events in progress or after the fact. Reduce dwell time and enable rapid response.

  • CCTV / video surveillance
  • Motion and PIR sensors
  • Door and window contact sensors
  • Glass break detectors
  • PACS access logs and audit trails
  • Environmental monitoring (smoke, water)
⚠️
Deterrent controls

Discourage an attacker from attempting an intrusion. Increase perceived risk of detection and apprehension.

  • Visible CCTV cameras
  • Security lighting
  • Warning and trespass signs
  • Uniformed security guards
  • Patrol vehicles and dogs
  • Alarm warning decals and panels
🔧
Corrective controls

Restore security after a breach has occurred. Limit damage and prevent recurrence of the same attack vector.

  • Security guard response and dispatch
  • Police liaison and handover
  • Emergency lock-down procedures
  • Access card revocation
  • CCTV footage preservation
  • Post-incident security review

Access Control Technology

Modern physical access control combines credential presentation with identity verification. Higher security zones require multiple independent factors — matching the Zero Trust principle of explicit verification applied to physical access.

Section 05

Surveillance & CCTV Architecture

A comprehensive CCTV system is both a deterrent and a detective control. Effective design ensures no blind spots, adequate resolution for identification, sufficient retention, and real-time monitoring integration.

📷
Camera types & placement
Match camera spec to the surveillance objective
PTZ (Pan-Tilt-Zoom): Deployed at perimeter and vehicle entry points. Operator-controlled or auto-tracking. Covers wide areas and can zoom to read number plates or capture facial detail at distance.
Fixed wide-angle: Lobbies, corridors, open office areas, car parks. Provides persistent coverage of high-traffic zones. Lower cost per camera than PTZ.
Dome (vandal-resistant): Internal high-risk zones, server rooms, secure areas. Concealed direction of view discourages targeted obstruction by intruders.
ANPR cameras: Vehicle entry and exit points. Read and log number plates automatically, cross-reference against approved vehicle register and law enforcement watchlists.
Covert cameras: High-theft areas, investigation support. Concealed in everyday objects. Requires legal review under GDPR/DPA for employee monitoring use cases.
🖥️
Video Management System (VMS)
Storage, monitoring, analytics, and integration
Storage: Minimum 31-day retention for most facilities; 90 days for secure sites. RAID-protected NVR or cloud storage. Encrypted at rest. Tamper-evident audit log on every access and export.
Video analytics: AI-driven motion detection, object classification (person / vehicle / package), loitering detection, perimeter trip-wire alerts, crowd density monitoring.
PACS integration: Camera associated with each access reader — when a card is swiped, footage from adjacent camera is automatically tagged with the credential used. Simplifies investigations.
SIEM integration: CCTV alerts (motion detection, camera tamper, offline camera) forwarded to the cyber SIEM as structured events for cross-correlation with logical access events.
Legal & privacy: Prominent CCTV signage required (GDPR/DPA in UK/EU). Data subject access request process. Footage access restricted to authorised personnel. Disposal procedure on retention expiry.
⚡
Intruder detection systems
Sensor types, zones, and grading
PIR sensors: Passive infrared detectors covering movement within a defined zone. Dual-technology (PIR + microwave) reduces false alarms. Grade 3 sensors required for high-risk premises.
Door and window contacts: Magnetic contacts detect opening of any protected aperture. Wired preferred over wireless for tamper resistance in secure zones.
Glass break detectors: Acoustic sensors tuned to the frequency of breaking glass. Covers multiple windows from one device. Used at ground-floor perimeter.
Vibration detectors: Detect physical attack on walls, safes, or cabinets. Used in high-security server rooms and media vaults to detect forced entry attempts.
Alarm grading (EN 50131): Grade 1 (low risk) to Grade 4 (high risk). Grade determines required sensor specification, signalling path redundancy, and response time contractual SLA.
🔗
Physical-cyber integration
Bridging the physical and logical security domains
PACS-SIEM feed: All access events (granted, denied, forced door, tailgate) forwarded to the SIEM. Correlate after-hours badge access with VPN logon from same user — confirms or rules out account sharing.
Impossible physical travel: User badged at London office 10 minutes after a cyber event registered at the Singapore office — flags either credential sharing or remote session compromise.
Insider threat detection: After-hours badge access to server room correlated with large data download from file server — UEBA detects the combination and escalates to SOC.
Unified incident response: Physical security operations and cyber SOC share a common incident platform — a physical intrusion alert and a simultaneous network anomaly are correlated into a single IR ticket.
ICS/OT protection: Physical security directly protects industrial control systems. Unauthorised physical access to a substation or SCADA panel can cause more damage than a cyber attack via the same path.

Data Centre Physical Security

Data centres require the highest level of physical security — a single physical breach can be more damaging than years of network-based attacks. Tier classification directly drives physical security requirements.

Section 07

Environmental Control Systems

Environmental threats — fire, flooding, power failure, extreme temperature — can cause more damage than a deliberate physical attack. Environmental controls are as essential as access controls for protecting critical infrastructure.

Incident Response Scenarios

Trigger: CCTV video analytics fires a perimeter intrusion alert at 02:14 on a Saturday. A person has climbed the outer fence at the north-east corner, away from the gatehouse. Motion detectors in the external lighting zone are also triggered. Walk through the full physical security response.
Trigger: PACS audit log shows an access event at the server room door that was preceded by a long door-open time (14 seconds) indicating a potential tailgate. The legitimate cardholder's access profile does not match the server room zone. CCTV at the door shows two people entering on one badge swipe.
Trigger: An employee reports a suspicious unattended package in the main reception area. The package is not addressed and the delivery has no record in the visitor management system. The building security manager receives the call and initiates the suspicious package response protocol.
